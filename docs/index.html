A Network-Constrained Conditional Randomization Analysis of Vehicle Collisions in Philadelphia, 2020–2024

Between 2020 and 2024, Philadelphia saw 36,303 traffic crashes. 620 were fatal, killing 647 people. Crashes declined while fatalities did not over the 5 years. This analysis asks whether the locations of fatal crashes carry information beyond the locations of crashes in general.

Methods

Inference is by conditional randomization. Each of 999 draws relabels 620 crashes as fatal, stratified by year so that the annual fatal totals and all crash locations are preserved. Under the null hypothesis that fatality is exchangeable among crashes, these draws generate the null distribution of any spatial statistic.

999 draws · 620 of 36,303

Results

Getis-Ord Gi* with false-discovery correction identifies 669 crash hotspots and 467 fatal hotspots, and the two sets overlap at a Jaccard index of 0.11. Random labeling produces an overlap of 0.13, p = 0.35. The fragmented fatal hotspot map is what 620 events among 36,303 look like under any labeling.

Volume-based targeting captures fatalities in proportion to crashes. The top 12% of network length carries 71.8% of crashes and 73.9% of fatal crashes. A ranking by crash volume locates fatal crashes exactly as well as it locates crashes and no better.

Results

Two statistics deviate from the null, and both deviations are small. First, fatal crashes occupy 2.40% of network length against a null of 2.05% [1.87, 2.23], p ≤ 0.001. Observed fatal crashes share segments less often, so they spread across more distinct segments than chance predicts.

Second, segments with a fatal crash in one year record another the following year at 5.3%, against a null of 1.8% [0.8, 3.1], p ≤ 0.001. A small set of locations recurs at three times the chance rate. 19 of every 20 fatal segments do not repeat.

The pattern

If the location of a fatal crash is close to random among crash locations, what does predict whether a crash kills? In these data the answer is street type. A crash on an arterial is roughly two and a half times as likely to be fatal as a crash on a residential street. The arterial classes together carry 47% of the city's crashes and 65% of its fatal crashes.

The pattern

Posted speed limits, where OpenStreetMap records them, show the same ordering. The lower rates at 35 and 45 mph rest on smaller samples and overlapping confidence intervals. The intervals at 25 and 40 mph do not overlap.

The pattern

The gradient is consistent with an impact-speed mechanism. Tefft (2013) estimates that the average risk of death for a struck pedestrian reaches 10% at an impact speed of 24.1 mph, 50% at 40.6 mph, and 75% at 48.0 mph. On streets designed for higher speeds, a crash is more often fatal.

Policy

Philadelphia recommitted to zero traffic deaths by executive order in March 2024 and released the Vision Zero Action Plan 2030 in November 2025, together with an updated High Injury Network built from KSI data. The city reports that under this network 12% of streets carry 80% of deaths and serious injuries. Three implications follow from the results above.

Limits

The randomization conditions on the observed crash locations. It answers where fatal crashes fall among crashes, not why crashes occur where they do. Exposure and traffic volume are therefore absent by construction, and road class is a coarse stand-in for street design.
